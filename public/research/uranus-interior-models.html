Ice giant interior composition models: Uranus and Neptune compared

Review of the three-layer interior models for Uranus, the constraints from Voyager 2 gravity and shape measurements, the metallic-superionic-water phase questions, and the prospects for refinement from a flagship Uranus orbiter.
2026-04-25 · 6 min read · Reviewed by Corgan Studio, Inc.

Introduction

Uranus and Neptune are the smallest and densest of the giant planets, with bulk compositions dominated by water, methane, and ammonia rather than the hydrogen-helium of Jupiter and Saturn. The interior structure of these "ice giants" is not directly constrained by detailed in-situ measurements - Voyager 2 provided only a single flyby of each. This report reviews the leading three-layer interior models for Uranus, the observational constraints, and the open questions.

Three-layer model

The standard interior model places three compositional layers in approximate hydrostatic equilibrium:

Outer envelope. Approximately 10-20 percent of total radius, composed of molecular hydrogen and helium with water-methane-ammonia minor constituents. The envelope is the layer where atmospheric dynamics and the visible cloud structure operate.

Mantle. Approximately 50-60 percent of total radius, composed predominantly of water, methane, and ammonia at high pressure (1 to 600 GPa). The mantle is the dynamo-generating layer; its electrical conductivity is dominated by ionic transport (H+, OH-, NH4+) rather than free electrons. The mantle's effective viscosity at relevant conditions is estimated at 10^7 to 10^9 Pa s, sufficient to support solid-state convection on geologic timescales.

Inner core. Approximately 20-30 percent of total radius, composed of rocky and metallic material at very high pressure. The core size is poorly constrained: it could be a small concentrated core or a diffuse rocky-metallic gradient extending into the mantle.

Voyager 2 gravity constraints

The Voyager 2 1986 flyby of Uranus measured the planet's gravitational moments J2 and J4 to first order during a single occultation track. The combined measurements constrain the mass-distribution moment integrals to approximately 5 percent precision, sufficient to discriminate broad classes of interior models but not to resolve fine compositional structure.

The measured J2 = 3.516 times 10^-3 implies a moderate-radius core with significant moment of inertia in the mantle. The measured J4 = -3.5 times 10^-5 places the planet on the dense-mantle, small-core branch of family solutions. Modern interpretations adopt an oblate-spheroidal model with rotation rate informed by the Voyager 2 magnetic-field 17.24-hour period.

Superionic water and metallic phases

At pressures above approximately 100 GPa and temperatures above 2000 K, water transitions to a "superionic" phase in which oxygen atoms form a fixed lattice while hydrogen ions are mobile and contribute to electrical conductivity. The superionic phase is predicted to occupy a significant pressure range in the Uranus mantle, with consequences for both the dynamo geometry and the heat-transport regime.

Laboratory experiments by Millot et al. (2019) and follow-on work have produced superionic water in shock-compressed samples and confirmed its existence at relevant Uranus-mantle conditions. The experiments support the inference of a thick superionic layer in Uranus and Neptune and constrain the parameter space of acceptable interior models.

Heat-flow anomaly

Uranus's measured intrinsic heat output is anomalously low - 1.06 +/- 0.08 times the absorbed solar flux, the lowest excess emission of any giant planet. Neptune, by contrast, has a normal excess emission of approximately 2.6 times absorbed solar. The Uranus deficit is unexplained.

Three classes of explanation have been proposed: (1) early-system stratification of the deep interior, locking heat below a stable composition gradient and isolating the visible atmosphere; (2) ongoing convective shutdown driven by the same compositional stratification; (3) a more complex giant-impact history that disrupted Uranus's interior structure compared to Neptune's. The 97.8-degree axial tilt is consistent with a substantial early impact.

Comparison with Neptune

Despite similar bulk masses (Uranus 14.5 Earth masses, Neptune 17.1 Earth masses) and bulk densities, Uranus and Neptune differ significantly in interior structure inferred from gravity moments and from the observed heat-flow asymmetry. Neptune's stronger heat output suggests a more vigorously convecting mantle with effective transport throughout. Uranus's weaker heat output suggests partial stratification. The two planets are therefore not simply scaled versions of each other; their interior histories are distinct.

Outlook

The 2023 Planetary Science Decadal Survey (Origins, Worlds, and Life) identified a flagship Uranus orbiter as the highest priority for new outer-planets exploration. The proposed mission carries a comprehensive geophysics package - gravity, magnetic, radio occultation, atmospheric sounding - to resolve interior structure to depth and longitude scales 100 times better than Voyager-era constraints. No Uranus mission is currently in implementation; the Decadal recommendation initiates the formulation phase, with possible launch in the early 2030s.
